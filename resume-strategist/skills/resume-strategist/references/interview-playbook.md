# Interview Playbook: Evidence-Based Questions for CTO Resume Extraction

## Overview

This playbook contains structured interview questions organized by dimension. The questions
are designed to extract high-impact, metrics-driven achievements from a CTO who leads product,
engineering, data, and infrastructure functions.

**Methodology**: Questions use the Socratic probing method — start broad, then drill into
specifics. Each question cluster moves from context → decision → action → measurable outcome.
This mirrors the CAR framework (Challenge → Action → Result) to naturally produce resume-ready
material.

**Interview discipline**: Ask 2-3 questions at a time via AskUserQuestion. Listen carefully.
Follow up on specifics. Do NOT move on until you have extracted at least one quantifiable
business outcome from each dimension.

**The Capital Rule** (from CTO Academy, 2025): "The two parts of the CV, the cover letter
and resume, aren't about you; they are about the organization and the role you are applying
for." Before optimizing the resume, you must "determine why that organization is even looking
for a candidate" for that role. The interview must uncover what the TARGET company needs,
then match the user's experience to those needs — not just list impressive achievements.

**The Balance Principle** (from Quantum Tech Resumes): A CTO resume must "demonstrate what
you have delivered with technology and the many ways in which you have changed the enterprise
for the better — without including so much technical detail that your resume makes the eyes
of the CEO glaze over from too much technical jargon." The interview should extract both the
strategic business vision AND the technical knowledge, calibrated to avoid either extreme.

## Phase 1: Company Context & Mission Alignment

These questions MUST come first. Without company context, individual achievements lack
strategic framing.

### Opening Questions (ask all three)

1. "Tell me about the company — what's the mission, what problem does it solve, and
   who are the customers?"
2. "What stage was the company at when you joined or took the CTO role? What stage
   is it now? (Revenue range, team size, funding stage)"
3. "What were the company's top 2-3 strategic priorities during your tenure?"

### Follow-up Probes

- "How did the board or CEO define success for the technology organization?"
- "What was the biggest existential risk to the company that technology had to address?"
- "Were there any pivots, M&A events, or major strategic shifts? What was your role?"
- "What was the competitive landscape? Where was technology a differentiator vs. table stakes?"

### What to listen for
- Specificity about mission (not just website copy but how the CTO internalized it)
- Connection between company priorities and technology decisions
- Inflection points where the CTO's work directly advanced the company's trajectory
- Revenue ranges, growth rates, team sizes that frame the scale of impact

## Phase 2: Business Outcomes Deep Dive

These questions are deliberately biased toward business metrics. For each answer, probe
until you get a number.

### Revenue & Growth Impact

1. "What product capabilities or technical decisions directly contributed to revenue growth?
   Can you estimate the revenue impact?"
2. "Did any technology work you led open new markets, segments, or geographies?
   What revenue did those new areas generate?"
3. "Were there deals you won (or competitors you displaced) because of technical
   capabilities you built? How many? What was the total pipeline value?"
4. "Did technology play a role in fundraising? Did investors cite specific technical
   capabilities as a reason for investment?"

### Cost & Efficiency Impact

5. "What were your biggest cost optimization wins? Infrastructure, people, tools, vendor?"
6. "How did you manage cloud spend as the company scaled? What was the spend trajectory
   vs. revenue growth?"
7. "Did you automate processes that previously required manual effort? How many
   person-hours per week/month did that save?"
8. "What was your most effective build-vs-buy decision? What did it save?"

### Speed & Time-to-Market

9. "How did you measure engineering velocity? What improvements did you achieve?"
10. "What was the deployment cadence when you started vs. when you left?
    (Monthly? Weekly? Daily? Multiple times per day?)"
11. "Can you think of a time when faster delivery directly impacted a business outcome?
    (Closed a deal, beat a competitor, captured a market window)"

### Risk & Reliability

12. "What was the platform reliability when you started vs. now? Uptime? Incidents?"
13. "What compliance certifications did you achieve? How did they unblock business?"
14. "What was the most significant security or reliability risk you mitigated?
    What was the potential cost if you hadn't?"

---

## Phase 3: Leadership & Organization Building

### Team Scaling

1. "Walk me through the engineering organization at the start vs. end of your tenure.
   How many people? What was the org structure?"
2. "What was your most important senior hire? Why? What changed after they joined?"
3. "How did you balance hiring speed with hiring quality? What was your acceptance rate?"
4. "How did you handle the transition from a flat org to a multi-level one?"

### Process & Culture

5. "What engineering processes did you establish that didn't exist before?
   What measurable impact did they have?"
6. "What was the retention rate on your team? How does that compare to industry?"
7. "How did you manage the tension between product pressure and engineering quality?"
8. "What was the hardest organizational or people decision you made? What did you learn?"

### Cross-Functional Leadership

9. "How did you partner with the CEO, CFO, or board on technology decisions?"
10. "Did you present to the board? How frequently? What topics?"
11. "How did you align product, engineering, data, and infrastructure when priorities conflicted?"
12. "Were you involved in M&A evaluation or integration? What was your role?"

---

## Phase 4: Technical Excellence (Business-Framed)

### Architecture & Platform

1. "What was the most important architectural decision you made? What were the alternatives?
   Why did you choose this path? What business outcome did it enable?"
2. "How did you approach technical debt? Was it a strategic decision or firefighting?"
3. "What's the scalability headroom of the platform you built?
   How far can it go before the next major investment?"

### Data & AI

4. "How did you establish data as a strategic asset at the company?"
5. "What AI/ML capabilities did you build? How did they translate to business value?"
6. "What data-driven decisions did your infrastructure enable that weren't possible before?"

### Infrastructure & Security

7. "What was your approach to cloud architecture? Multi-region? Multi-cloud? Why?"
8. "How did you handle security? What's the security program maturity level?"
9. "Were you involved in preparing for investor due diligence from a technology perspective?"

## Probing Techniques

When the user gives a vague or unquantified answer, use these follow-up patterns:

### The Specificity Probe
> "You mentioned you 'improved deployment speed.' Can you be more specific? What was
> the deployment frequency before? After? How long did the transformation take?"

### The Business Impact Probe
> "That's a strong technical achievement. How did it translate to business impact?
> Did it affect revenue, costs, customer satisfaction, or the sales process?"

### The Scale Probe
> "Help me understand the scale — how many engineers were involved? How many
> customers did this affect? What was the data/transaction volume?"

### The Comparison Probe
> "How does that compare to what you inherited? Or to industry benchmarks?"

### The Estimation Helper
When the user says "I don't know the exact number":
> "Let's estimate. How many customers were affected? What's the average revenue
> per customer? Even a rough order of magnitude helps — was this a $100K impact,
> $1M, or $10M?"

**Integrity check**: If the user cannot recall or estimate with any confidence, note this
as a gap. The bullet should use qualitative framing ("significant," "material") or ranges
rather than a fabricated specific number.

## Red Flags During Interview

Watch for these patterns and address them directly:

| Pattern | What to say |
|---------|-------------|
| Only talks about one function | "You've shared strong engineering examples. What about product decisions? Data? Infrastructure?" |
| Lists responsibilities, not achievements | "That tells me what you were responsible for. What CHANGED because of what you did?" |
| Technical-first framing | "That's technically impressive. How did the board or CEO see this? What business problem did it solve?" |
| No numbers | "Every claim is stronger with a number. Even approximate — was this a small, medium, or large impact?" |
| All individual contributions | "Did you build or lead a team that did this? At the CTO level, your impact is through organization, not just personal delivery." |
| Understating impact | "You seem to be describing this modestly. From the outside, leading a team of X to achieve Y at Z scale is significant. Let's make sure the resume reflects the real magnitude." |
