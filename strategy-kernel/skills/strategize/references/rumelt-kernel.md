# The Strategy Kernel

Richard Rumelt's core framework from *Good Strategy Bad Strategy* (2011). The kernel is the essential logical structure that all good strategy must have.

## The Three Elements

### 1. Diagnosis

An explanatory judgment about the nature of the challenge being faced.

**What it is:**
- Simplifies the overwhelming complexity of reality by identifying certain aspects as critical
- An INTERPRETATION that defines the terrain of action, not merely a list of facts
- The leader absorbs complexity and ambiguity and passes the organization a simpler, solvable problem
- A creative, active act of judgment — choosing what matters and framing the challenge

**Tests for a good diagnosis:**
- SIMPLIFICATION: Does it reduce complexity to something actionable?
- SPECIFICITY: Does it name specific obstacles, not general conditions?
- EXPLANATORY POWER: Does it explain WHY the situation is the way it is?
- ACTIONABILITY: Does it point toward what kind of response is needed?
- NOT A GOAL: Is it describing reality, not describing desires?

**Critical distinction:** A diagnosis is NOT a goal. It is an understanding of "what is going on here." It often reframes the situation in a way that makes the path forward visible.

**The most common cause of bad strategy is a weak or absent diagnosis.**

### 2. Guiding Policy

An overall approach chosen to cope with or overcome the obstacles identified in the diagnosis.

**What it is:**
- Like guardrails on a highway — directs and constrains action without fully defining it
- Rules out a vast array of possible actions (this is where the power of choice enters)
- NOT a goal or vision — it is a method, an approach, a direction
- Analogous to a scientific hypothesis — an educated bet about what approach will work
- Often embodies a specific advantage or source of leverage

**Tests for a good guiding policy:**
- RESPONDS TO DIAGNOSIS: Does it directly address the diagnosed challenge?
- MAKES CHOICES: What does it rule out? (if nothing, it's not a real policy)
- CHANNELS ACTION: Does it point toward specific kinds of moves?
- NOT A GOAL: "Dominate the market" is a goal; "Compete on integrated user experience" is a policy
- UNCOMFORTABLE TRUTH: If everyone agrees, it probably isn't making hard choices

### 3. Coherent Actions

A set of feasible, coordinated policies, resource commitments, and maneuvers designed to carry out the guiding policy.

**What they are:**
- Must be COHERENT — coordinated with each other and mutually reinforcing
- Must be FEASIBLE — concrete steps, not wishes
- Includes resource allocation, organizational changes, specific initiatives
- "Strategy is visible as coordinated action imposed on a system"

**Tests for coherent actions:**
- COORDINATION: Do actions work together, not at cross-purposes?
- REINFORCEMENT: Does each action make the others more effective?
- CONSISTENCY: Do any actions contradict the guiding policy?
- COMPLETENESS: Is anything missing that the policy requires?
- FEASIBILITY: Can these actually be done with available resources?

**Red flag:** If the actions read like a grab-bag of unrelated initiatives, they are bad strategic objectives (Hallmark 4).

## How the Three Interrelate

The kernel is a LOGICAL CHAIN:
1. The diagnosis defines the challenge
2. The guiding policy provides the approach to that challenge
3. The coherent actions implement the guiding policy

Each element constrains and gives meaning to the others:
- A guiding policy that does not respond to the diagnosis is disconnected
- Actions that are not guided by the policy are incoherent
- A diagnosis without a response is just analysis

**The connection test:** "Because [diagnosis], our approach is [guiding policy], which we implement through [actions]." If this sentence doesn't flow naturally, the kernel is broken.

**Foundational quote:** "The core of strategy work is always the same: discovering the critical factors in a situation and designing a way of coordinating and focusing actions to deal with those factors."
