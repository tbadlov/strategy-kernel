# Thinking Like a Strategist

From Part III of *Good Strategy Bad Strategy*. The cognitive discipline and mental models of effective strategic thinking.

## Strategy as Hypothesis

Rumelt treats strategy as a HYPOTHESIS — not a plan, not a goal, but an educated bet about what will work. Like scientific hypotheses, strategies should be:
- Tested against reality
- Revised when evidence contradicts them
- Held with appropriate confidence (not certainty)

**Implication:** A strategy should always be accompanied by: "What would prove this wrong?" and "What early signals should we watch for?"

## Anomaly as Starting Point

Good strategic thinking often starts with noticing an ANOMALY — a gap between what you observe and what you would expect.

**Questions that reveal anomalies:**
- Why does this market exist with no premium player?
- Why is this competitor succeeding despite apparent disadvantages?
- Why hasn't anyone done X yet?
- What explains this gap between performance and potential?

**Example:** Howard Schultz noticed there were no upscale products in American fast-food/coffee — this anomaly led to Starbucks.

Anomalies are the raw material of strategic insight.

## The Create-Destroy Process

Rumelt recommends a deliberate process of generating and then attacking your own strategic ideas:

1. **Generate multiple alternatives** — do not fixate on the first idea
2. **Actively try to DESTROY your own ideas** — look for flaws, test assumptions, stress-test against scenarios
3. **Imagine a panel of experts** scrutinizing your proposed solution with hard questions:
   - Why should we do this?
   - What is wrong with this approach?
   - How does it fit with overall trends and dynamics?
   - How could this fail?
4. **The idea that survives destruction** is more likely to be sound

**Key insight:** Most people generate one idea and then rationalize it. The strategist generates multiple ideas and tries to kill them.

## Metacognition — Thinking About Thinking

A critical skill of the strategist is METACOGNITION: the ability to think about your own thinking process, recognize your biases, and make judgments about your own judgments. Rumelt describes this as making "judgments about judgments" and considers it the most useful shift in viewpoint.

**Common biases to watch for:**
- **Anchoring:** Fixating on the first number or idea encountered
- **Status quo bias:** Preferring the current state because change feels risky
- **Herd instinct:** Following what others are doing instead of thinking independently
- **Sunk cost fallacy:** Continuing because of past investment rather than future value
- **Confirmation bias:** Seeking information that supports your existing view
- **Optimism bias:** Overestimating the probability of favorable outcomes

**Practice:** After developing a strategy, explicitly ask:
- Am I rationalizing rather than reasoning?
- What evidence have I ignored or downweighted?
- Would I make the same choice if I were starting fresh?
- What would the smartest critic say about this?

## The Strategist as Problem-Solver

Rumelt is emphatic that strategy is fundamentally PROBLEM-SOLVING, not aspiration-setting.

**The strategist's job:**
1. Identify the most critical challenge
2. Speak truth to power — confront executives with the actual competitive, technological, or managerial challenges
3. Find the pivot point where focused effort will produce the greatest effect
4. Choose what NOT to do (choice and focus are the essence)

**The starting point is always "what is the challenge?" not "what do we want?"**

## Key Examples: Good Strategy

| Example | Diagnosis | Guiding Policy | Key Lesson |
|---------|-----------|---------------|------------|
| **Apple 1997** | Sprawling product lines, failed innovation, 90 days of cash | Radical simplification | Cut to 4 products (2x2 matrix), eliminated ~4,100 jobs. The kernel in action. |
| **Desert Storm** | Iraqi forces dug in along front lines | Envelopment via unexpected flank | Schwarzkopf's "left hook." Good strategy is often unexpected. |
| **Nvidia** | 3D graphics cards were the future | Shift from multimedia to focused GPU development | Implemented unique 3-team development cycle. Stock grew 21x. |
| **Walmart** | KMart's size and decentralized structure were weaknesses | Centralized, networked hub system | KMart's cultural inertia prevented response. |
| **IBM under Gerstner** | Problem was failing to USE integrated capabilities | Integration around customer solutions | Not a hardware company — a solutions company. |

## Key Examples: Bad Strategy

| Example | Hallmark | Lesson |
|---------|----------|--------|
| **Chad Logan's "20/20 plan"** | Goals as strategy | Set 20% revenue growth and 20% margins as "strategy" — no diagnosis, no approach |
| **LAUSD school district** | Bad strategic objectives | Failed to face the actual challenge of education quality |
| **Pacific Northwest city** | Bad strategic objectives | 47 "strategies," 178 action items, item 122: "Create a strategic plan" |
| **International Harvester** | Incoherent objectives | Failed strategic plan illustrating disconnected actions |
| **GM post-Sloan** | Entropy | Carefully designed brand positioning decayed into internal competition |

## Strategy Development Process Summary

1. **Start with the challenge** — What is really going on here?
2. **Diagnose** — Simplify reality to its critical elements
3. **Generate alternatives** — Multiple guiding policies, not just one
4. **Destroy** — Stress-test each alternative ruthlessly
5. **Select** — Choose the approach that survives scrutiny
6. **Design actions** — Coordinated, coherent, feasible
7. **Identify proximate objective** — The first feasible target
8. **Treat as hypothesis** — Monitor, test, revise
