# The Four Hallmarks of Bad Strategy

Rumelt is emphatic: bad strategy is NOT merely the absence of good strategy. It is an active presence — the result of specific dysfunctions.

## Hallmark 1: Fluff

A restatement of the obvious, combined with a generous sprinkling of buzzwords that masquerade as expertise. Uses inflated, abstract language to create the illusion of high-level thinking.

**Detection test:** Strip away the buzzwords and jargon. Is there any actual content remaining? Fluff uses "Sunday words" (words that are inflated and unnecessarily abstruse) to hide the absence of thought.

**Examples:**
- "Our strategy is to leverage our core competencies to deliver synergistic value propositions"
- "We will drive transformational change through our innovative ecosystem"

## Hallmark 2: Failure to Face the Challenge

A strategy that does not define or acknowledge the challenge being faced. If you do not identify and analyze the obstacles, you do not have a strategy.

**Detection test:** Can you state the specific challenge being addressed? If the strategy doesn't name what obstacle it is overcoming, it isn't a strategy.

**Rumelt quote:** "A strategy is a way through a difficulty, an approach to overcoming an obstacle, a response to a challenge. If the challenge is not defined, it is difficult or impossible to assess the quality of the strategy."

## Hallmark 3: Mistaking Goals for Strategy

Statements of desire (revenue targets, growth ambitions, market share goals) presented as if they were strategies. Simply being ambitious is not a strategy.

**Detection test:** Is this a statement of ambition or an actual approach? "Grow revenue 20%" is a goal. "Win the enterprise segment by offering integrated compliance tooling that mid-market competitors cannot match" is closer to strategy.

**How it manifests:** "Most corporate strategic plans are simply three-year or five-year rolling budgets combined with market share projections. Calling this strategic planning creates false expectations that the exercise will somehow produce a coherent strategy." — Rumelt

**Template-style strategy** is a specific sub-pathology: filling in blanks for vision, mission, values, and then generating laundry lists of lofty but unactionable goals.

## Hallmark 4: Bad Strategic Objectives

A long list of "things to do," often mislabeled as "strategies" or "objectives," that fail to address critical issues or are impractical. Rumelt calls this a "dog's dinner" of objectives.

**Detection test:** Is this a focused set or a grab-bag of unrelated items? Good strategy works by focusing energy on one, or a very few, pivotal objectives whose accomplishment leads to a cascade of favorable outcomes.

**Example:** A Pacific Northwest city had 47 "strategies" and 178 action items — item 122 was "Create a strategic plan."

## Root Causes of Bad Strategy

### Unwillingness to Choose
Bad strategy often arises because leaders refuse to make hard choices, attempting to accommodate all conflicting demands. Strategy requires saying no to some stakeholders.

### Universal Buy-In as Warning Sign
If a strategy receives universal agreement, this is a warning sign — it likely means no difficult decisions have been made. Good strategy makes some people uncomfortable.

### Template Thinking
Applying a fill-in-the-blanks approach (vision/mission/values) that produces the appearance of strategy without its substance. The template becomes a substitute for actual strategic thinking.

### The Positive-Thinking Fallacy
The mistaken belief that desire and willpower alone are sufficient to produce results. "If we just believe hard enough and set ambitious goals, we'll get there." This is not strategy.

## Additional Red Flags

- No mention of obstacles, competition, or tradeoffs
- Strategy that reads like a budget with aspirations attached
- Confusing operational effectiveness with strategy
- Anchoring on solutions before understanding the problem
- Long lists without prioritization or logical connection
- No explanation of WHY this approach will work
