# Nine Sources of Strategic Power

From Part II of *Good Strategy Bad Strategy*. Each is a source of strategic advantage that can be leveraged.

## 1. Leverage

Strategic leverage arises from a mixture of:
- **Anticipation** — of where effort will be most effective
- **Pivot points** — natural or created points of concentrated effect
- **Concentrated application** of effort

Coordination itself provides the most basic leverage — aligning multiple efforts toward the same point. The strategist's job is to find where focused effort will produce disproportionate results.

## 2. Proximate Objectives

An objective that is close enough at hand to be feasible. The more uncertain and dynamic the situation, the more proximate the objective should be.

**Key example:** JFK's call to put a man on the moon sounded audacious but was actually a carefully chosen proximate objective — US engineers had already developed most of the required technology through the ballistic missile program. The objective was feasible, galvanizing, and focused.

**Key insight:** A good proximate objective names a target that the organization can reasonably hit, or even overwhelm. A leader who sets an objective too far beyond current capabilities is not leading — they are dreaming.

**Application:** Among your actions, identify the FIRST DOMINO — the proximate objective whose achievement creates momentum for everything else.

## 3. Chain-Link Systems

A system has chain-link structure when its performance is limited by its weakest link. Each component depends on the others such that if one underperforms, the entire system suffers.

**Strategic implications:**
- Excellence in a chain-link system creates DURABLE advantages because a competitor must match excellence at EVERY link simultaneously (extremely difficult)
- Investing in strengthening one link while another remains weak is wasteful
- Identify whether your strategy has chain-link dependencies — if so, ALL links must be strong

**Classic example:** IKEA's low-cost, self-assembly, flat-pack, suburban-warehouse model is a chain-link system where each element reinforces the others. Copying one element without the others does not work.

## 4. Design

Strategy as design means creating a tightly integrated, coherent whole rather than a collection of independent parts. Like physical design, the parts must fit together. The elements are chosen and arranged to produce a specific, desired effect.

**Application:** A well-designed strategy is one where all elements work together and reinforce one another. Test by asking: if you remove one element, does the whole thing fall apart? If so, you have good design.

## 5. Focus

Concentration of resources and attention on a limited number of objectives.

**Rumelt:** "Strategy is mainly about focus" and "the deeper meaning of focus — a concentration and coordination of action and resources that creates an advantage."

**Focus means saying no.** It is the deliberate choice to pursue some things and not others, directing resources where they will have the greatest impact. It is NOT about doing many things efficiently; it is about choosing to do fewer things.

## 6. Growth

Rumelt is skeptical of growth-as-strategy:
- **Healthy growth:** Results from growing demand, superiority in a field, or expanded capabilities
- **Unhealthy growth:** Growth by acquisition is often bad strategy — buying the appearance of growth without creating new value

**Application:** If "growth" appears in your strategy, interrogate it. Is growth the result of a sound strategy, or is it being mistaken for a strategy itself?

## 7. Using Advantage

An advantage is the result of asymmetries — differences between rivals.

**Types of competitive advantage:**
- Superior access to resources
- Better product or service
- Position-based advantage (network effects, installed base)
- Greater efficiency

**To strengthen an advantage:**
- Deepen it (get even better at what you're good at)
- Broaden it (apply advantage to adjacent areas)
- Create higher demand for your offerings
- Strengthen isolating mechanisms (barriers preventing competitors from replicating)

## 8. Dynamics (Riding Waves of Change)

Waves of change are largely exogenous — beyond any single organization's control. The strategist's job is to understand the forces underlying the main effect to discover second-order effects — those are where the strategic opportunities lie.

**Five guideposts for riding waves of change:**

1. **Rising fixed costs** — shifts power toward larger competitors who can amortize costs
2. **Deregulation** — opens new competitive frontiers and disrupts incumbents
3. **Predictable biases** — incumbents tend to react in predictable, often defensive ways
4. **Incumbent response patterns** — large organizations respond slowly and in characteristic ways
5. **Attractor states** — an industry's likely resting point once the wave passes; focus resources toward that anticipated end state

**Application:** What waves of change affect your situation? What are the second-order effects? Where will the industry settle?

## 9. Inertia and Entropy

### Inertia (resistance to change)

Three types:
1. **Inertia of routine:** "The way things are done here." Deeply embedded processes that resist change, often disrupted only by external shocks.
2. **Cultural inertia:** Social behaviors and shared meanings deeply rooted and resistant to change even when the environment demands it.
3. **Inertia by proxy:** When an organization chooses not to pursue a viable future because the current situation remains too profitable (the classic incumbent's dilemma).

### Entropy (drift toward disorder)

The natural tendency of organizations to become less organized and less focused over time unless actively managed. Without vigorous management attention, organizations drift toward disorder.

**Classic example:** Under Alfred Sloan, GM had a tightly coordinated product line where each brand occupied a distinct price point. Over subsequent decades, without active management discipline, the brands drifted into overlapping positions, competing with each other — textbook organizational entropy.

**Application in strategy:**
- Assess your OWN organization's inertia — what changes will it resist?
- Assess COMPETITOR inertia — what prevents them from responding to your moves?
- Watch for entropy — is your organization drifting from its strategic focus?

## Sources of Power Audit Framework

When developing or evaluating strategy, assess each source:

| Source | Currently Used? | Missed Opportunity? | Competitor Threat? |
|--------|----------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Leverage | How/where? | Where could effort concentrate? | Where are they focused? |
| Proximate Objectives | Is first target feasible? | Could target be more proximate? | What are they targeting? |
| Chain-Link Systems | Are all links strong? | Could you create chain-link advantage? | Do they have chain-link? |
| Design | Is strategy integrated? | Could elements fit tighter? | Is their design coherent? |
| Focus | Resources concentrated? | Spreading too thin? | Where are they focused? |
| Growth | Healthy or acquisitive? | Better growth path? | How are they growing? |
| Advantage | What asymmetry? | Deeper/broader? | What's their asymmetry? |
| Dynamics | Riding what wave? | Second-order effects? | How will they respond? |
| Inertia/Entropy | What resists change? | Competitor inertia to exploit? | Are you drifting? |
